## Overview

This document presents a **closed-loop Cooling Credit model** that connects restaurants, hotels, school meal services, food factories, supermarkets, municipalities, humus/composting operators, and farmers.

The model converts food waste, kitchen scraps, fallen leaves, and other organic residues into humus or compost, then returns them to farmland, gardens, green spaces, or local food-production systems.

The core idea is simple:

```text
Restaurants and food businesses
↓
Food waste, vegetable scraps, organic residues, fallen leaves
↓
Humus or composting operators
↓
Soil amendment returned to farmers
↓
Food production
↓
Restaurants and local consumption
```

This is not merely a waste-management model. It is a soil, water, vegetation, and natural-cooling recovery model.

---

## 1. Why Humus Production Can Become a Cooling Credit Model

Organic-waste humus production can contribute to cooling in several ways.

```text
Reduced incineration
↓
Reduced combustion heat and waste-treatment burden

Humus production
↓
Recovery of soil organic matter

Soil organic matter recovery
↓
Improved water retention and microbial activity

Improved water retention
↓
Suppression of surface-temperature rise

Plant growth and evapotranspiration recovery
↓
Restoration of natural cooling capacity
```

Cooling Credits should evaluate not only CO2 reduction, but also actions that reduce actual heat load in regions, cities, soils, forests, farms, and ecosystems.

Humus production can be evaluated as a short-term reduction in incineration, a medium-term recovery of soil water retention, and a long-term recovery of vegetation, evapotranspiration, and microbial circulation.

## 3. Basic Flow

### Step 1: Separate Organic Residues

Restaurants and food businesses separate suitable organic materials such as:

- Vegetable scraps
- Fruit peels
- Tea leaves
- Coffee grounds
- Rice and bread residues
- Cooking residues
- Fallen leaves
- Pruned branches
- Other organic materials

Materials containing excessive salt, oil, meat, fish, bones, packaging, plastics, metals, or glass should be managed according to local processing standards and should not be treated as universally suitable for humus production.

---

### Step 2: Build a Collection Route

A regular collection route connects food businesses to humus or composting operators.

Recorded items should include:

- Business name
- Collection date
- Collected weight
- Separation quality
- Contamination rate
- Transport distance
- Vehicle type
- Storage conditions

---

### Step 3: Produce Humus or Compost

Collected organic materials are processed according to local conditions.

Management items include:

- Moisture
- Temperature
- Aeration
- Carbon-nitrogen balance
- Contaminant removal
- Fermentation status
- Odor
- Maturity
- Sanitary conditions

Immature organic material should not be returned directly to farmland.

---

### Step 4: Return the Material to Soil

Completed humus or compost is supplied to farmers, urban farms, parks, green spaces, or forest-regeneration sites.

Receiving sites should record:

- Application area
- Application amount
- Soil moisture
- Crop or plant type
- Surface temperature
- Vegetation condition
- Yield
- Changes in soil organic matter

---

### Step 5: Return Food to Local Consumption

Farmers produce food using regenerated soil, and part of that food can return to restaurants or local consumers.

This completes the closed loop:

```text
Restaurant
↓
Food waste
↓
Humus production
↓
Farmland
↓
Agricultural products
↓
Restaurant
```

This connects food circulation, local economies, soil regeneration, and heat-load reduction.

## 5. Cooling Contributions That Can Be Evaluated

### 5.1 Reduced Incineration

By diverting food residues and organic waste away from incineration, the system can reduce combustion heat and waste-treatment burden.

### 5.2 Recovery of Soil Water Retention

Humus can improve soil structure and water retention.

Improved soil water retention can help suppress rapid surface-temperature rise.

### 5.3 Recovery of Plant Evapotranspiration

When soil moisture and organic matter recover, plant growth and evapotranspiration may recover.

Evapotranspiration is one of nature's cooling functions.

### 5.4 Recovery of Microbial Circulation

Returning organic matter to soil can support microbial networks and restore soil vitality.

This becomes a foundation for long-term natural cooling capacity.

### 5.5 Formation of Local Circulation

Circulating organic resources locally can reduce waste transport, external fertilizer dependence, and treatment burden.

## 8. Risks and Cautions

### 8.1 Sanitation Risk

Food residues require careful sanitary management.

Potential issues include odor, pests, pathogens, contamination, and immature compost.

### 8.2 Salt and Oil

Restaurant leftovers may contain salt and oil.

They may not be suitable for farmland unless properly separated and processed.

### 8.3 Contamination

Plastic, metal, glass, and packaging contamination can degrade humus or compost quality.

Clear separation rules and inspection systems are required.

### 8.4 Transport Burden

Long-distance transport can reduce the benefit of the system.

Local or short-distance circulation should be prioritized.

### 8.5 Legal and Regional Rules

Food-waste handling, composting, and farmland application may be subject to local laws, permits, and sanitation standards.

These must be checked before formal operation.

## 9. Small-Scale Pilot Roadmap

### Phase 1: One Restaurant and One Farmer

- Begin separating food residues.
- Connect to a humus or composting operator.
- Return mature humus or compost to a farmer.
- Return part of the harvest to the restaurant.

### Phase 2: Multiple Restaurants

- Expand to a shopping street or local restaurant group.
- Create a collection route.
- Collect monthly data.

### Phase 3: Municipality, Schools, Hotels, and Supermarkets

Expand to:

- School meals
- Hotels
- Public facilities
- Supermarkets
- Food factories

### Phase 4: Cooling Credit Score Estimation

Enter data such as incineration reduction, soil moisture change, surface temperature change, vegetation change, transport burden, and MRV quality into the estimator.

## 10. Complementarity with the Center-Mist Ultrasonic Cooling Fan

The Center-Mist Ultrasonic Cooling Fan is a short-term cooling technology.

The food-waste-to-humus closed loop is a medium- to long-term natural cooling recovery system.

```text
Short term: reduce immediate heat using mist cooling
Medium term: restore soil and water cycles through humus production
Long term: recover vegetation, microbes, and evapotranspiration
```

Together, they expand Cooling Credits from device-based cooling to social-system-based cooling.

## 11. Summary

Food-waste and organic-waste humus production is one of the most accessible implementation candidates for Cooling Credits.

The reason is simple: restaurants, food businesses, humus/composting operators, farmers, and municipalities already exist. The main task is to connect them into a measurable loop.

This model can support food-loss reduction, incineration reduction, soil regeneration, agricultural regeneration, local economies, and natural cooling recovery at the same time.

The closed loop is easy to understand:

```text
Restaurant
↓
Food waste
↓
Humus production
↓
Farmer
↓
Agricultural products
↓
Restaurant
```

This makes it a strong social implementation model for explaining Cooling Credits.

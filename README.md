## Overview

**Cooling Credit Implementation Portfolio** is an open implementation framework for organizing the practical technologies, industrial entry points, and social systems that can support the **Cooling Credit** concept.

Cooling Credits are not limited to carbon accounting. They evaluate measurable contributions to actual heat-load reduction, water-cycle restoration, soil moisture recovery, vegetation transpiration, waste-heat reduction, ocean circulation recovery, and urban cooling.

The central thesis of this repository is simple:

> **Cooling Credits should not be a system only for large corporations or governments.  
> By integrating existing technologies, natural cooling principles, urban design, agriculture, ocean technologies, mobility systems, and everyday cooling devices, any company can participate in direct planetary cooling.**

Carbon credits mainly evaluate carbon accounting.  
Cooling Credits evaluate thermal-environment accounting.

In other words, climate action should not ask only how much CO₂ has been reduced.

It should also ask:

- Has local heat stress actually been reduced?
- Has surface temperature been lowered?
- Has urban waste heat been reduced?
- Has soil regained its ability to hold water?
- Has vegetation transpiration recovered?
- Has the water cycle been restored?
- Can ocean surface heat or vertical circulation be improved safely?
- Has heat risk in daily life been reduced?
- Have the environmental conditions for civilizational survival improved?

The purpose of Cooling Credits is to institutionalize these measurable cooling contributions.

## Background: Why an Implementation Portfolio Is Needed

Climate policy has long focused on reducing CO₂ emissions.  
This remains necessary and will continue to be essential.

However, the damage caused by global warming ultimately appears as **heat**.

Extreme heat, urban heat islands, droughts, forest fires, rising sea surface temperatures, marine heatwaves, stronger typhoons, localized extreme rainfall, rising cooling demand, crop damage, and accumulated urban waste heat are not only issues of atmospheric CO₂ concentration.

They are also issues of heat accumulated inside the Earth system.

Therefore, climate action needs two directions.

| Category | Function | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Input-side measures | Reduce future heat trapping | CO₂ reduction, energy efficiency, renewable energy, carbon fixation |
| Output-side measures | Reduce already accumulated or stagnant heat | Water-cycle restoration, urban cooling, soil moisture recovery, vegetation transpiration, ocean circulation, waste-heat reduction |

Cooling Credits are a framework for institutionalizing the second category: **output-side heat-load reduction**.

To spread output-side measures throughout society, it is necessary to clarify what kinds of actions can count as cooling contributions.

That is the role of this implementation portfolio.

## Implementation Candidate 1: Center-Mist Ultrasonic Cooling Fan

A **center-mist ultrasonic cooling fan** is a device concept that sprays ultrasonic mist from the center of a fan or blower and combines airflow with fine water droplets to produce evaporative cooling.

When water evaporates, it absorbs heat from its surroundings.  
By using this natural phenomenon, the device may reduce local heat stress.

Possible applications include:

- outdoor work
- agricultural work
- event venues
- shopping streets
- storefronts
- schools and parks
- factories and warehouses
- disaster shelters
- dry-region living environments
- heatstroke countermeasures

Possible evaluation indicators include:

- air temperature reduction
- perceived temperature reduction
- WBGT reduction
- cooling effect per unit of electricity
- cooling effect per unit of water
- heatstroke-risk reduction
- local cooling area

A basic estimation formula is:

```text
Q_evap = m_evap × L_v
```

- Q_evap: evaporative cooling amount
- m_evap: mass of evaporated water
- L_v: latent heat of vaporization of water

## Implementation Candidate 2: Organic-Waste Humus Conversion and Soil Cooling

Food loss, kitchen waste, fallen leaves, pruning residues, agricultural residues, and other organic waste are often treated as waste in modern society.

In nature, however, organic matter is a circulating resource.

Organic waste can be dried, crushed, sanitized when necessary, and decomposed by microorganisms into humus-like material.  
This material can then be returned to fields, forests, and degraded soils.

Expected effects include:

- soil moisture recovery
- microbial-environment recovery
- reduction of chemical-fertilizer dependence
- promotion of organic farming
- surface temperature reduction
- restoration of plant transpiration
- carbon fixation
- ecosystem regeneration
- recovery of cooling functions in farmland and forests

Dry soil tends to accumulate heat.  
Moisture-retentive soil can buffer heat more effectively.

Thus, soil regeneration is not only related to food production.  
It is also related to planetary cooling.

Cooling Credits can evaluate such actions as efforts to restore soil as a cooling system.

## Implementation Candidate 3: OBS — Deep-Sea Aeration and Ocean Breathing

**OBS (Ocean Breathing System)** is a concept for deep-sea or mid-depth aeration that aims to help the ocean “breathe” by sending air or oxygen into deeper layers and supporting dissolved oxygen recovery and ocean metabolism.

The ocean is the largest heat reservoir on Earth and a central component of the climate system.

Rising surface temperatures, marine heatwaves, oxygen depletion, red tides, dead zones, and stagnating ocean circulation affect both global thermal balance and marine ecosystems.

OBS may be evaluated through indicators such as:

- dissolved oxygen recovery
- support for ocean vertical circulation
- restart of ocean metabolism
- improvement of oxygen-depleted waters
- support for non-red-tide plankton systems
- marine ecosystem recovery
- assistance in moving or redistributing surface heat

Ocean interventions must be handled with caution.

Regional suitability, ecological risk, stop conditions, and long-term monitoring are essential.

---

## Implementation Candidate 4: OTU — Ocean Tuning Unit

**OTU (Ocean Tuning Unit)** is a conceptual ocean unit designed to monitor and tune ocean surface heat, vertical circulation, oxygen supply, plankton-support conditions, and marine-heatwave risks.

OTU may serve the following purposes:

- reduction of ocean surface heat
- support for vertical circulation
- assistance in heat exchange with mid-depth or deeper water
- dissolved oxygen recovery
- plankton support
- marine-heatwave risk reduction
- theoretical moderation of El Niño-related risks
- support for marine ecosystem recovery

It is important not to claim that OTU can certainly stop El Niño.

A responsible positioning is:

> OTU may theoretically contribute to suppressing or moderating El Niño-related risks by reducing ocean surface heat, supporting vertical circulation, and improving marine cooling and oxygenation conditions.  
> However, verification, numerical modeling, long-term monitoring, ecological safety, and stop conditions are indispensable.

---

## Implementation Candidate 5: UMS — Ultrasonic Mist System

**UMS (Ultrasonic Mist System)** refers here to an ultrasonic mist cooling system that uses ultrasonic vibration to turn water into fine mist and disperse it into the air for evaporative cooling.

UMS can be applied to:

- urban mist systems
- heat countermeasures for roads and plazas
- intake-side pre-cooling for air-conditioner outdoor units
- factories and warehouses
- localized cooling in dry regions
- initial cooling for desert-regeneration models
- heat mitigation around farmland
- cooling support for outdoor workers
- disaster shelters and temporary facilities

UMS should not be evaluated only by temperature reduction.

Humidity increase and WBGT change must also be evaluated.

Water-based cooling may be effective in dry regions.  
However, in humid regions, it may worsen perceived heat stress by increasing humidity.

Therefore, Cooling Credit assessment should include:

- air temperature reduction
- WBGT reduction
- humidity-risk increase
- water consumption
- cooling efficiency
- sustainability of water sources
- rainwater or reclaimed-water utilization rate

---

## Implementation Candidate 6: Plant Transpiration, Forest Restoration, and Agricultural Regeneration

Plants do more than absorb CO₂.

They draw water from their roots and release it through their leaves as transpiration.  
This process removes heat from the surrounding environment.

Forests, street trees, farmland, grassland, wetlands, orchards, and mixed vegetation systems are also natural cooling devices.

Evaluation targets include:

- canopy cover
- vegetation index
- transpiration amount
- soil moisture
- surface temperature reduction
- surrounding air temperature reduction
- biodiversity
- carbon fixation
- estimated contribution to cloud formation or rainfall

Forest and agricultural regeneration should be evaluated not only as carbon fixation, but also as cooling contribution.

---

## Implementation Candidate 7: UHV — Mobility Cooling and Waste-Heat Reduction

**UHV (Ultimate Hybrid Vehicle)** is a mobility concept that combines waste-heat reduction, water utilization, dry-region adaptation, and energy-efficiency improvement.

Modern vehicles and transport infrastructure consume energy and emit heat.  
In cities, vehicles, roads, parking lots, air conditioners, buildings, and transport infrastructure overlap and intensify heat accumulation.

If mobility systems can reduce waste heat, lower surrounding heat loads, and use water and air more efficiently, they can also become part of cooling contribution.

Evaluation targets include:

- vehicle waste-heat reduction
- fuel or electricity efficiency improvement
- cooling-water or mist-use efficiency
- impact on urban heat load
- applicability in dry regions
- mobility-based cooling contribution

## Cooling Credit Evaluation Model

Cooling Credits should be evaluated through a combination of multiple indicators, not by a single simplified metric.

A basic structure can be expressed as follows:

```text
Cooling Credit Score =
  Thermal Reduction
+ Water Cycle Recovery
+ Evapotranspiration Recovery
+ Heat Risk Reduction
+ Energy Demand Reduction
+ Ecological Cooling Recovery
- Water Stress Penalty
- Humidity Risk Penalty
- Ecological Risk Penalty
```

The important point is that side effects must also be evaluated.

For example, mist cooling may be effective in dry regions but may worsen WBGT in humid regions.  
Afforestation may pressure groundwater if performed in water-limited regions.  
Ocean-circulation technologies must be carefully assessed for ecological impacts.

Cooling Credits should not be a simple system of “anything that cools is good.”

They must incorporate regional conditions, measurability, safety, and sustainability.

## Conclusion

Cooling Credits do not deny the need for CO₂ reduction.

Rather, they institutionalize action against the heat that has already accumulated and remains insufficiently addressed by carbon accounting alone.

Their greatest advantage is that any company, any region, and any technological field can participate in planetary cooling.

Everyday devices, agriculture, forests, cities, oceans, mobility systems, and civilizational frameworks can all be connected to the shared purpose of cooling the Earth.

Cooling Credits are an implementation system for embedding direct planetary cooling into society and increasing the possibility of civilizational survival.
